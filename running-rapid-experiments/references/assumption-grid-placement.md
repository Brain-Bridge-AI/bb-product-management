# Assumption Grid — Placement Rules

The 2×2 in the center of the Experiment Loop. Axes:

- **Horizontal: Known ↔ Unknown** — how much real evidence exists for this assumption.
- **Vertical: Crucial ↔ Not Crucial** — cascading risk / lead-domino weight.

The upper-right quadrant (Crucial + Unknown) is where the LOFA lives.

## How to place on the Known / Unknown axis

Ask: how much *real evidence* do we have that this assumption is true?

| Placement | Signal |
|---|---|
| Far left (Known) | Rich direct experience, current customer data, market evidence, or prior successful experiments |
| Mid-left | Informed estimate from analogous products or customer types |
| Center | Educated hypothesis from secondary research or one or two customer conversations |
| Mid-right | Extrapolated from a different domain |
| Far right (Unknown) | No direct evidence; pure assumption |

**Placement is relative to the other assumptions on the canvas, not absolute.** Score against your own set. A mid-stage company with lots of customer data might have nothing in the "Far right" zone; a brand new product might have nothing in the "Far left" zone.

## How to place on the Crucial / Not Crucial axis

Ask: if this assumption is false, what downstream damage does it do?

The metaphor is **lead domino** — some assumptions are upstream of others. If the upstream one fails, the downstream ones don't matter, because you won't get that far.

| Placement | Signal |
|---|---|
| Top (Crucial) | If false → business model does not work at all. Everything collapses. |
| Upper-mid | If false → a core pillar (pricing, channel, retention) breaks |
| Middle | If false → significant redesign needed, but business continues |
| Lower-mid | If false → minor adjustment |
| Bottom (Not Crucial) | If false → small operational tweak |

## Cascading-risk analysis — the LOFA test

The upper-right quadrant is the shortlist. To pick the single LOFA from that quadrant, test each candidate for **cascade**: if you answer this one assumption, how many *other* assumptions get de-risked or answered as a side effect?

**Example cascade (from the elicited case):**

Candidate: "Customers will understand our token-based pricing model."
- If TRUE → pricing model stays. Revenue assumptions survive. Education-cost concerns resolve. Channel pitch simplifies. Multiple boxes de-risked.
- If FALSE → pricing must change. Revenue assumptions reshape. Channel pitch needs a different hook. Cascade of changes.

Candidate: "Fractional CFOs (vs. full-time CFOs at roofing companies) are the better segment."
- If TRUE → segment narrows. Channels pivot to fractional-CFO networks.
- If FALSE → broader segment remains. Channels unchanged.
- Less cascade.

The first candidate has stronger cascade — answering it moves more of the canvas. It's the LOFA.

**If no candidate in the upper-right has strong cascade, re-rank.** You may have the wrong assumptions on the grid, or the grid may be crowding assumptions that aren't really crucial.

## Common placement errors

- **Placing a "nice to know" assumption in Crucial.** If failure doesn't crash the business, it's not Crucial. Force rank — only one assumption can be the *most* crucial.
- **Placing everything in Unknown because the team lacks confidence.** Calibrate relative to the other post-its. Even if every assumption feels shaky, some are shakier than others.
- **Letting complexity signal craft.** An assumption that's hard to articulate isn't inherently Crucial. Simplify the sentence first; then place.
- **Avoiding the scary one.** Teams sometimes place the actual LOFA in the mid-Crucial zone because testing it would be painful or expensive. Call this out. The LOFA is supposed to be scary — that's why it's the one to test.

## When the grid feels "empty" in the upper-right

If you've brainstormed and nothing lands in the upper-right:

- Re-check the brainstorm. Are you missing customer-behavior assumptions? These are the hardest to generate but usually the most crucial. Walk the Lean Canvas Customer / Problem / Solution boxes again with fresh eyes.
- Check whether you're suppressing the scary ones. Ask: "what would kill this business by Q4?" The answer is an assumption, and it probably goes top-top-right.
- Consider whether the product is too early to experiment on. If truly nothing rises — i.e., no crucial-unknown assumption exists — the more likely answer is that the team has done too little customer discovery to know what's risky. Route back to populating-lean-canvas or dedicated customer-discovery work.

# The Bowling Pin Test

Borrowed from Geoffrey Moore, *Crossing the Chasm*. The core idea: you don't attack a whole market at once. You pick one narrow segment where you can realistically be the #1 choice in 18 months — the first "bowling pin" — and you dominate it. Then you knock down adjacent pins one at a time.

This reference helps the skill narrow segments to the right size. Use it in Section 3 of the report.

## Too fat, too thin, just right

### Too fat ❌

- "SMBs"
- "CFOs"
- "Companies that use AI"
- "Businesses with under 500 employees"

These are not segments. They're demographic slices. Anyone selling to them is competing with 500 other tools. You can't be the #1 choice because there's no coherent group of customers who would all ask "who's the best at solving MY specific version of this?"

### Still too fat ⚠️

- "Small businesses that use AI tools"
- "CFOs who manage variable costs"
- "Roofing contractors"

Better. There's a shared trait. But the group is still heterogeneous enough that different companies in it have different problems, different buying patterns, and different workarounds. You'd have to position differently for each sub-group. That's not a segment, that's a market.

### Right-sized ✅

- "Fractional sales leaders helping 10-50-person roofing contractors in TX/FL who are losing storm-season jobs to faster-quoting competitors"
- "CFOs at 50-200 person B2B SaaS companies who've been asked to approve AI tool spend for the first time in the last 6 months"
- "Ops directors at meat-processing plants running USDA audits without a compliance software system"

The shared trait is specific enough that everyone in the group has (roughly) the same problem at the same time. They read the same content, attend the same conferences, trust the same consultants. A single pitch deck works for all of them.

### Too thin ⚠️

- "CFOs named Jay at fractional advisory firms in Chicago who own orange sweatshirts"

If there are fewer than ~50 companies or people globally who fit the definition, the segment is too thin to be a business. Narrow is good; vanishingly small is a hobby project.

## The bowling pin test — two questions

When you look at the segment definition, ask:

1. **Could you realistically be the clear #1 choice for this specific group within 18 months?**
   - If yes → pin is the right size or smaller. Good.
   - If no → pin is too fat. Narrow further.

2. **If you dominated this pin, would the next adjacent pin be visibly reachable?**
   - Moore's attack sequence: the pins are connected. Customers in pin 1 refer customers in pin 2. Pin 2 trusts pin 1. You want that chain.
   - If the "next pin" isn't obvious, you've picked an isolated segment with no expansion story. Fine for a first test, problematic for a business.

## How to narrow

If a segment is too fat, try adding one of these dimensions until it passes the #1-choice test:

- **Job-to-be-done** — what specifically are they hiring a solution FOR? ("Storm-chase billing" not "billing.")
- **Trigger event** — what happened recently that makes NOW the right time? ("Just hired their first ops manager," "Just lost a big deal to a faster competitor.")
- **Company size band** — not "SMB," but "10-50 employees."
- **Geography** — not "US," but "TX/FL/GA." (Especially relevant for services/industry-specific segments.)
- **Technographic** — "Already using AccuLynx but not HailTrace."
- **Buying-committee shape** — "Where the fractional leader has direct sign-off authority from the owner."

Add dimensions until the segment passes the test. Stop when the segment is still big enough to matter (see the sizing section of the main SKILL.md).

## Worked example — narrowing a real segment

**Start:** "Small businesses that need help with AI."
- Too fat. Every SaaS company says this. Not a pin.

**Add job-to-be-done:** "Small businesses that want AI help for their back-office operations."
- Better. But "back office" still covers dozens of distinct problems.

**Narrow the job:** "Small businesses that want AI help for their customer operations — quoting, scheduling, follow-up."
- Getting there. But still 5 industries deep.

**Narrow the industry:** "Roofing contractors who want AI help for their customer operations — quoting storm jobs, scheduling crews, following up with insurance adjusters."
- Good. A clear shared problem. A clear vocabulary. A single pitch.

**Add size + trigger:** "10-50-person roofing contractors who've had a bad storm season in the last 12 months and are losing jobs to faster-quoting competitors."
- Now we have a pin. #1-choice in 18 months is plausible. Next pin is visible (restoration contractors with similar dynamics).

## When the segment is just too big for anyone to dominate

Sometimes the segment you want doesn't exist at the narrowness required — the problem is too broad, or the buyers are too heterogeneous. When that happens, the bowling-pin test tells you the truth: **this is not a startable segment right now.**

Options:
- Pick a different segment that does pass the test.
- Serve a narrower pin first, then expand.
- Wait until you have the resources to attack from multiple directions (this is the Amazon playbook, not the startup playbook).

Don't fake the narrowness by over-specifying in ways that lose real customers. Narrow for power, not for cuteness.

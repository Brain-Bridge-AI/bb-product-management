# Data Sources for Segment Research

What each public data source produces, what it costs, and what it cannot tell you. Pick based on the specific claim you need to defend.

## For sizing (how many are there?)

### US Census Bureau — County Business Patterns & Economic Census
- **What it gives you:** Company counts by NAICS code, employment size, and geography. Free.
- **URL:** census.gov/programs-surveys/cbp.html
- **Best for:** "How many [NAICS industry] companies are there in [state/county]?"
- **Limitations:** Updated annually (lag of ~1 year). Doesn't capture sub-industry granularity.

### Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS) — OES, QCEW
- **What it gives you:** Employment counts, wages, and job-title concentration by industry and geography. Free.
- **URL:** bls.gov
- **Best for:** "How many [job title] work in [industry] in [region]?"
- **Limitations:** Job-title-level data lags. Fractional/contractor work undercounted.

### IBISWorld
- **What it gives you:** Industry reports with revenue size, growth rates, major players, cost structure, trends. Paid (~$1,000+ per report, sometimes available via library access).
- **Best for:** Revenue pool sizing. "Is this a $500M industry or a $50B industry?"
- **Limitations:** US-focused primary reports; sub-segment definitions often too broad.

### Statista / IBISWorld / Grand View Research
- **What they give you:** Market sizing reports, often cited in press. Paid, mixed quality.
- **Best for:** Consensus size estimates for pitching or strategic docs.
- **Limitations:** Often recycle each other's numbers. Treat with skepticism; cite source and vintage.

## For firmographic filtering (who specifically?)

### LinkedIn Sales Navigator
- **What it gives you:** Filter companies by industry, headcount, geography, revenue band, growth signal, recent funding, tech stack (partial). Paid (~$100/mo).
- **Best for:** Building a named target list. "Show me all 10-50-person roofing companies in TX with a leadership change in the last 12 months."
- **Limitations:** Industry tags are noisy; headcount bands are broad; no behavioral data.

### Apollo.io / ZoomInfo / Clearbit
- **What it gives you:** B2B contact and firmographic data with decent tech-stack coverage and trigger signals (hiring, funding, new execs). Paid.
- **Best for:** Export-ready target lists with decision-maker emails and tech stack.
- **Limitations:** Contact accuracy varies by segment; tech-stack data has gaps.

### Crunchbase
- **What it gives you:** Funded startups with funding stage, investors, headcount, recent rounds. Free basic, paid pro.
- **Best for:** Venture-backed segments and tech-adjacent categories.
- **Limitations:** Skewed toward SF/NY tech; misses bootstrapped, traditional, or services businesses.

### G2, Capterra, TrustRadius
- **What they give you:** Software buyer reviews with buyer titles, company size, industry, and feature requests.
- **Best for:** Technographic sanity checks ("who actually uses AccuLynx, and what do they say?"), and finding the observable-behavior signal "switched from X to Y."
- **Limitations:** Heavily skewed to software categories; sample bias toward vocal users.

## For behavioral and trigger signals

### Job boards (Indeed, LinkedIn Jobs, industry-specific)
- **What they give you:** What companies are hiring, for what roles, with what stack mentions. Free to search.
- **Best for:** Behavioral signals — "this company is hiring their first ops manager" or "this company just posted a Salesforce admin role."
- **Limitations:** Job descriptions lag by 30-90 days.

### 10-Ks, S-1s, annual reports
- **What they give you:** How public companies describe their market, customers, competitors, and risks, in their own language.
- **Best for:** Picking up the *language* customers use about the problem; identifying market size figures the company itself endorses.
- **Limitations:** Only public companies; skewed to mature/large.

### Industry association rosters
- **What they give you:** Member lists with company names, often with contact info.
- **Best for:** Directly reachable named targets. Often the fastest path from "I need 10 prospects in this segment" to "here are 10 prospects in this segment."
- **Limitations:** Not every industry has a real association; some are pay-to-list.

### Podcasts and trade publication guest lists
- **What they give you:** Names of people who actively discuss the problem publicly.
- **Best for:** Warm-intro candidates for interviews. These people already self-selected as "willing to talk about this topic."
- **Limitations:** Represents the vocal minority, not the median buyer.

## For community / reachability

### Reddit (specific subreddits), Slack communities, Discord, specialized forums
- **What they give you:** The words customers use about the problem, ranked by community engagement.
- **Best for:** Voice-of-customer language, unprompted pain points, existing workarounds.
- **Limitations:** Community biases; may not represent buyers.

### LinkedIn groups and hashtags
- **What they give you:** Professional communities around a topic.
- **Best for:** Finding target audiences that explicitly self-identify as belonging to a segment.
- **Limitations:** LinkedIn's group features are underused; signal is thin.

### Conferences and trade events
- **What they give you:** Attendee lists (sometimes), speaker lists (public), exhibitor lists (public).
- **Best for:** Finding the companies willing to spend to be in the room — a strong proxy for "this segment is real and has budget."

## How to cite

Every sizing or filtering claim in the report should have a source and a date in parentheses. Example:

> "There are approximately 12,000 residential roofing contractors in TX with 10-50 employees (Census CBP, 2023)."

If you're using a paid source the user doesn't have access to, still cite it — they can verify or request access.

## When to stop using desk research

Stop when you have:
1. A defensible count estimate (within an order of magnitude).
2. A specific filter stack (NAICS + headcount + geography + behavioral trigger) that would produce a list of real targets.
3. 5-10 named candidate interviewees.
4. A list of what you still don't know.

Anything beyond that is procrastination. Go interview.

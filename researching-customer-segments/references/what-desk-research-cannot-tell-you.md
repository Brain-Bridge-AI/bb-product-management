# What Desk Research CANNOT Tell You

Every segment research report must include this list, customized to the segment. Its purpose is simple: force the user back to customer interviews and experiments. If the user tries to use the desk research to skip interviews, this section is the hard stop.

## The questions only customers can answer

Public data gives you the shape of the segment. It cannot give you any of these:

### 1. Is this problem actually painful for them?

You can count companies with a plausible problem. You cannot know how much it hurts. The difference between "this problem annoys them once a quarter" and "this problem keeps them up at night" is not in any public source. It's in the customer's voice.

### 2. Are they actively trying to solve it?

Desk research can spot workarounds (bought a tool, hired someone, posted a job). It cannot distinguish "they're actively trying to solve this" from "they bought something three years ago and forgot about it."

### 3. Would they pay for your specific solution?

Desk research can surface existing spend in adjacent categories. It cannot tell you whether the specific solution you're proposing would unlock a willingness to buy. Only the customer can reveal that — and often only after they've tried something.

### 4. What would they need to see to switch?

Incumbents win on inertia. The bar for someone to leave their current workaround is high and specific. Desk research cannot reveal the exact switching threshold — only interviews and experiments can.

### 5. How do they actually make the decision?

Buying-committee shape (CFO + Ops Director + champion) is partially visible in public data. The *actual* decision process — who vetoes, what gets asked in week 3 of the evaluation, what kills a deal — is not.

### 6. What language do they use about the problem?

You can scrape Reddit and G2 reviews for vocabulary. What you cannot get is the specific phrases a customer uses to describe the problem to their boss, because those phrases are said out loud in meetings, not typed into forums.

### 7. What's the real competing alternative?

Not the category ("CRM"), not the named competitor ("HubSpot"), but the thing the customer *would actually do instead* of buying from you. Often it's "nothing" or "keep using Excel." Desk research cannot reveal this — it requires asking "walk me through what you'd do tomorrow if we didn't exist."

### 8. How urgent is it?

The segment may have a real problem with a real budget. That's not the same as "they'll buy this quarter." Urgency is a customer-specific signal that only reveals itself in a sales conversation or a pilot.

## The rule

**Anything in this list that the user wants an answer to requires talking to real customers.** Route them to `running-problem-interviews` to prep those conversations. The segment research is a map; the interviews are the territory.

## What to put in the report's Section 5

Pick the 3-5 questions from above that are most load-bearing for this specific segment and canvas, and list them as a short section. Prefix with: "This research cannot tell you:" — so the user can't miss it.

Example for a roofing-contractor segment:

```
## What this desk research CANNOT tell you

- How painful the "can't forecast storm-season costs" problem actually is, in their own words.
- Whether they'd pay for a variable-cost forecasting tool, or if they'd stick with gut-feel.
- What specific workaround they use today, and what it would take to switch.
- Who actually has to sign off on software spend in a 10-50 person roofing shop.
- Whether this is a quarterly annoyance or a weekly blocker.

To answer these, run 5 problem interviews with candidates from Section 4. See `running-problem-interviews`.
```

## If the user pushes back

If the user says "can we skip the interviews?" or "can you just tell me if this is a good bet?", the answer is no. Say:

"I can tell you the segment is the right size and shape. I cannot tell you whether the customers actually have the problem, would pay for your solution, or would switch from what they're doing today. Those answers only come from real conversations. The research in this report is the pre-work — it makes the interviews shorter and sharper. It doesn't replace them."

Then route to `running-problem-interviews`.

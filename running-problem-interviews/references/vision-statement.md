# Vision Statement (Opener)

The opener is the first 60-90 seconds of the call. It has one job: anchor the customer on their own world so they can orient themselves to what you want to learn, without priming their answers with your product.

## Format

"I believe I can help **[specific customer segment]** **[specific outcome]**."

Two variables. No product noun. No feature list. No mention of the solution.

## Rules

- **Segment must be specific.** "Small businesses" is too broad. "Fractional sales leaders at small roofing contractors" is specific enough to let the customer see themselves in it.
- **Outcome must be about them, not about you.** "Reduce the negative impact of the feast-and-famine cycle" describes a change in their life. "Sell more AI tools" describes your revenue.
- **No product noun.** The moment you say "an AI platform" or "a billing system" or "a tool that does X", you've shifted from vision to pitch.
- **Aspirational is fine.** "I'm still figuring this out" or "I'm just getting started" signals humility and reduces the customer's pressure to evaluate you as a seller.

## Worked examples

**Good:**
- "I believe I can help fractional sales leaders at small businesses bring AI into their operations without runaway costs."
- "I believe I can help roofing contractors forecast their storm-season variable costs more accurately."
- "I believe I can help CFOs at 50-200 person companies get predictable AI spend without giving up flexibility."

**Bad (product-nouns or vague):**
- ❌ "I'm building an AI platform for small businesses." (product noun)
- ❌ "I'm working on tools to help people." (vague segment + outcome)
- ❌ "I'm creating a variable-pricing billing system with token-based metering." (full feature pitch)

## When to skip the formal vision statement

For **follow-up interviews with someone the user has already met**, the formal vision statement can feel redundant or even awkward. Swap for a warm restatement of purpose:

- "I'm working on something new and I want to learn from you, not pitch. I have nothing to sell today — I'm going to hammer you with questions and hope you'll give me your direct and honest feedback."

This is what the user did in the elicitation session with a known interviewee. It worked — it signaled humility, confirmed no-pitch, and invited directness. Use this pattern for any second-or-later conversation.

## What comes after the vision statement

After delivering the vision statement, immediately:

1. Ask for recording consent: "Okay if I record so I can pay attention instead of taking notes?"
2. (Optional) One warm-up sentence of rapport — comment on the weather, thank them for the time, reference a shared moment.
3. Go straight into Q1.

Don't linger on the vision. It's an anchor, not a speech.

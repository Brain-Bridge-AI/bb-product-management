# Question Design Checklist

Every question in a problem interview script must pass all of these rules. One-page reference, walk through it during the edit pass.

## The rules

### 1. Open-ended only — never yes/no

Yes/no questions produce yes/no answers. The interview is learning, not a survey.

- ❌ "Do you have trouble forecasting variable costs?"
- ✅ "Tell me about how you forecast variable costs at your company."

### 2. No multiple-choice inside questions

Listing categories inside a question ("utilities, payment processing, cloud, anything") anchors the customer on your options and turns the question into a survey. Ask the question bare and let them fill it.

- ❌ "Tell me about a software or service where the cost moves up and down based on usage — utilities, payment processing, cloud, anything like that."
- ✅ "Tell me about a software or service where the cost moves up and down based on usage."

### 3. Past behavior only — never predicted future behavior

People are terrible at predicting their own future behavior. They will give you an answer, and that answer will be unreliable. Past behavior is the real signal.

- ❌ "Would you pay for a tool that solved this?"
- ❌ "How would you use something like this?"
- ✅ "Tell me about the last time you bought a tool to solve this kind of problem. How did that go?"

### 4. Story-first

Get them narrating a specific past situation before asking anything abstract. Abstract questions get rehearsed answers; stories expose real behavior.

- ❌ "How do you think about software purchases?"
- ✅ "Walk me through the last software purchase you made. What happened?"

### 5. Use "why" liberally

Every surface-level answer has a deeper cause. "Why did you do that?" / "Why did that matter?" / "Why was that the choice?" — keep asking until you hit something concrete.

### 6. Include "how do you solve this today?" when pain surfaces

Uncovers existing alternatives, workarounds, and switching costs. Often reveals that what sounded like a painful problem has an acceptable workaround, which changes the LoFA verdict.

- ✅ "When that happens, how do you handle it today?"
- ✅ "What do you do right now when [pain] comes up?"

### 7. No leading questions

Don't bake your hypothesis into the question. Let the customer's answer stand or fall on its own.

- ❌ "This must be really painful for you, right?"
- ✅ "How does that affect you?"

### 8. No summarizing assertions mid-interview

Summarizing pressures the customer to agree or push back. Use sparingly, and only when deploying the deliberate-incorrect-paraphrase technique (see `signal-detection.md`).

- ❌ "So it sounds like cost is the biggest issue for you."
- ✅ "What's the biggest issue for you when it comes to [thing]?"

## Edit-pass checklist

Walk through the script and check each question against all 8 rules:

- [ ] Open-ended, not yes/no
- [ ] No multiple-choice enumerations
- [ ] Past behavior, not predicted future
- [ ] Story-first where possible
- [ ] "Why" follow-ups drafted
- [ ] "How do you solve this today?" included where pain surfaces
- [ ] No leading language
- [ ] No summarizing assertions

If a question fails, rewrite it. If you can't rewrite it without breaking the flow, cut it — three clean questions beat five compromised ones.

# Leading Experiment Reviews

## What this skill is for

Your team has just finished a cycle of work — usually a rapid experiment testing a Leap of Faith Assumption — and they're about to present the results to you. This skill is about how you show up in that conversation.

The core principle: **your job is to poke holes in the team's experiment, bring wider-lens evidence the team doesn't have, keep the team moving fast, and clear the path for the next cycle.** Your job is NOT to re-litigate the business model, take the decision back, or rescue the team when they're still working the problem.

## The leader's four jobs in a team share-out

### 1. Poke holes in the experiment — not the business model

Your team's job is to test the business model. Your job is to test whether they tested it well.

Ask about the rigor of what they did:
- "What was the riskiest assumption you were testing? Was it the riskiest, or just the easiest?"
- "What's the evidence? Not opinions, not compliments — what did customers actually DO?"
- "What would have changed your mind? Did you go in with a falsifiable hypothesis?"
- "Did you test with 3-5 real customers, or did you talk to one person who already liked the idea?"
- "What surprised you?"

Do NOT use the share-out to re-open strategy, pricing, segment, or vision. Those are your responsibility to set and defend. Mixing them in collapses the team's ownership of the experiment and teaches them to bring you the questions you already know the answer to.

See `references/poking-holes-in-experiments.md` for the question bank and common team moves to watch for.

### 2. Bring the wider-lens evidence the team doesn't have

You see things the team doesn't. Other teams. Market context. Historical patterns. Regulatory signals. Competitor moves. A customer conversation you had last week. Something the board said.

When the team's conclusion depends on an assumption you have contrary evidence for, tell them. Concretely. With the source.

- "That lands differently when you know that [customer segment] just [moved / bought / cut] in [area] last quarter."
- "Another team tested something similar six months ago and hit this same wall — here's what they learned."
- "Regulatory just shifted on [thing]. That changes your risk ordering."

Do this as a peer bringing data, not as an authority overriding the team. Your wider view makes the team's next cycle sharper; it doesn't invalidate this one. Let them integrate it and decide.

**Pre-review prep (optional but useful):** before the share-out, consider pulling a summary from `capturing-customer-insights` on the segment the team is working in. Ten minutes of read-mode across prior insights (especially anything tagged `Contradicts: [assumption-id]` for the LoFA in play) gives you something concrete to bring. Not required — your existing knowledge is usually enough — but the repo compounds across teams and cycles, and the insights there are the cheapest wider-lens evidence you have.

### 3. Keep them moving fast

Rapid experiments are rapid. If your team is building a 6-week experiment to test whether customers understand a pricing page, something is wrong.

Watch for overbuilding:
- Experiments designed to be statistically significant instead of directionally informative.
- Mockups that become prototypes that become MVPs before any customer has weighed in.
- Long written briefs instead of 3-5 customer conversations.
- "We need to launch the real thing to test this" — no you don't.

Your job is to call this out and reset the team to the smallest, fastest test that would give meaningful evidence. The speed is the point. See `references/watching-for-overbuild.md` for patterns.

### 4. Clear the path for the next cycle

By the end of the share-out, the team should know what they're doing next and what's in their way. Your job is to remove whatever is in the way that only you can remove.

- Decisions that need executive sign-off.
- Budget, tools, access.
- Permission to kill a zombie project.
- Guardrails for the next experiment.
- Intros to customers the team can't reach alone.

You leave the meeting owning action items. The team leaves owning the next cycle's decision. If you leave owning the decision and they leave owning your to-do list, you've inverted the relationship.

## What you do NOT do

Pulled from the canon (Edmondson, Heifetz, Marquet, Scott, Cagan, Ries) — these are the common failure patterns.

- **Rescue.** Don't jump in with the answer the moment the team wobbles. Let them work the problem.
- **Solution.** Don't reflexively suggest "have you tried X?" when they're still presenting. Ask instead.
- **Accept opinion as evidence.** When the team says "customers loved it," ask what they DID, not what they said.
- **Take the decision back.** If they ran the experiment, they own the Persevere / Pivot / Stop call. You sign off, you don't replace it.
- **Mistake silence for agreement.** If the room nods too fast, mine for what they're not saying.
- **Make it unsafe to share bad news.** If they ran a failing experiment, thank them for running it cleanly. How you react to bad news determines what they'll bring you next time.

## The six questions

If you have one minute before a share-out, memorize these. They do 80% of the work.

1. **What was the riskiest assumption you were testing?**
2. **What did customers actually DO?** (Not say, not promise — do.)
3. **What surprised you?**
4. **What would have changed your mind?**
5. **What's blocking you from the next cycle?**
6. **What do you need from me?**

Full question bank in `references/six-questions-expanded.md`.

## After the share-out: your commitments

The share-out isn't done when the team stops talking. It's done when you've named what you're going to do differently based on what you heard.

- What decision did you make (or defer) as a result?
- What are you going to stop doing that you used to do before you trusted the team's evidence?
- What did you commit to removing from their path by when?

Write these down. Share them with the team. If you don't follow through, the next share-out is theater.

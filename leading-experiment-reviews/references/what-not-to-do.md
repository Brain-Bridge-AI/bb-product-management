# What NOT To Do

The six failure patterns a leader falls into during a team share-out. Each one is easy to catch yourself on if you know what you're looking for. The fix in every case is the same family of move: ask instead of tell, protect the messenger, give the work back.

---

## 1. Rescuing

**What it looks like:** Team is halfway through presenting, hits a rough spot, and you jump in with the answer. "Oh — what you want to do is X." The team relaxes because you took over. The problem is now yours.

**Why it's bad:** Teams that get rescued stop trying to solve hard problems. They just bring you the rough spot and wait. Next time they'll bring you a smaller problem, and the time after that an even smaller one.

**Catch-yourself cue:** you feel the urge to fix something mid-sentence. That urge is the rescue impulse.

**Fix:** "Keep going — what's the next step you're considering?" Let them finish. Then ask instead of tell.

---

## 2. Advice monster

**What it looks like:** "Have you tried X?" "You should do Y." "What about Z?" You're in brainstorm mode, they're in present mode. Every advice bullet you fire is a question you could have asked instead.

**Why it's bad:** advice from the leader lands as an instruction, even if you meant it as a suggestion. The team now has an extra 6 items to pursue and no clarity on which are priorities.

**Catch-yourself cue:** you're about to say "have you tried..."

**Fix:** turn it into a question. Not "have you tried X?" but "what have you tried?" Nine times out of ten they've already considered X, and you didn't need to suggest it. The one time they haven't, you'll learn more from their reason than from pushing X.

Borrowed from Michael Bungay Stanier, *The Coaching Habit*.

---

## 3. Accepting opinion as evidence

**What it looks like:** Team says "customers loved it." You nod. You move on. You let that statement count as validation for the next decision.

**Why it's bad:** "Customers loved it" is words. Experiments test what customers DO. Words and actions diverge all the time. If you accept words, you'll green-light bets that collapse under real customer behavior.

**Catch-yourself cue:** you hear "love," "excited," "interested," or "definitely would."

**Fix:** "What did they do after they loved it?" Force the team to produce a behavior. If there is none, the experiment didn't test demand.

Borrowed from The Mom Test (Fitzpatrick) and Torres.

---

## 4. Taking the decision back

**What it looks like:** Team presents results and asks "what should we do?" You answer. Meeting ends.

**Why it's bad:** the team ran the experiment. They have the context. They own the Persevere / Pivot / Stop call. When you take it back, you teach them to present and wait, not to present and decide.

**Catch-yourself cue:** the team asks "what do you think we should do?"

**Fix:** "Based on what you just told me, what do YOU think you should do?" Hear their answer. Then your job is to sign off (or push back), not to replace their thinking.

Borrowed from Marquet (intent-based leadership) and Cagan (empowered teams).

---

## 5. Mistaking silence for agreement

**What it looks like:** Team presents a conclusion. You ask "any concerns?" Nobody raises a hand. You take that as alignment.

**Why it's bad:** silence in a hierarchical setting is almost never agreement. It's usually "I don't know if it's safe to say no" or "I haven't thought about it enough to push back." Either way, you're shipping without the team's real buy-in.

**Catch-yourself cue:** the room nodded too fast.

**Fix:** "What are we not saying in this room?" Or: "If this decision turns out wrong in six weeks, what will we say we should have caught today?" Both surface the thing nobody wanted to raise.

Borrowed from Lencioni (mine for conflict) and Edmondson (psychological safety).

---

## 6. Making it unsafe to share bad news

**What it looks like:** Team ran an experiment and it failed. They tell you. You react with frustration, disappointment, or a question that implies they should have known better. Next time, they won't bring you the bad news.

**Why it's bad:** how you react to the first piece of bad evidence determines how much evidence you'll ever see. Teams that sense punishment around failure filter their reports. You end up with a rosy view that doesn't match reality.

**Catch-yourself cue:** you're frustrated or disappointed when results come back negative.

**Fix:** Thank them for running the experiment cleanly. Separate "we got a negative result" from "they did something wrong." A well-run failed experiment is a win — you just saved months of building the wrong thing. Say that out loud.

Borrowed from Edmondson (*The Fearless Organization*, intelligent failure).

---

## The universal fix

All six failures share one repair move: **when you're about to offer something — an answer, an advice bullet, a correction — turn it into a question first.**

- "Have you tried X?" → "What have you tried?"
- "You should do Y." → "What do you think you should do?"
- "That's not good enough." → "What evidence would make this more conclusive?"
- "This is wrong." → "Walk me through how you got here."

The question keeps ownership with the team. The answer takes it back.

## When to break the rule

Not every moment is a coaching moment. If the team is blocked by a decision only you can make — a budget approval, an executive sign-off, cross-team permission — give the answer. Don't hide behind coaching form when what they need is an authority call.

The rule is: ask first, answer second. Not: ask only, never answer.

# Watching for Overbuild

Rapid experiments are rapid. The most common failure mode is a team that turns a 5-day test into a 5-week project. Your job is to spot the drift and reset them fast.

## What overbuild looks like

Watch for these patterns during the share-out or in prep conversations.

### The prototype is becoming a product

They started with a paper mockup. By week two it's a clickable prototype. By week three there's a working backend. No customer has weighed in yet.

**Reset:** "Before we build any more — what's the one thing we still don't know about the customer? Can three conversations this week answer it?"

### The experiment got designed to be statistically significant

Team is planning 200 users, A/B split, 95% confidence. Rapid experiments don't need this. They need directional signal from 3-5 real customers.

**Reset:** "This is a hypothesis test, not a launch. If three out of five customers do X, we move forward. If zero do it, we kill it. What's the smallest version that gets us that?"

### The team is writing briefs instead of talking to customers

Lots of strategy docs, canvases, assumption maps, competitive analyses. No customer quotes in the deck. No "we talked to five people and here's what they said."

**Reset:** "Stop writing. Who can you talk to this week? Five conversations, past behavior stories, no pitch. Bring me what you hear on Friday."

### The test requires launching the real product

"We can't really test this without building the full integration." Usually false. Almost every experiment can be faked — concierge MVPs, Wizard-of-Oz, landing pages, paper prototypes, manual backends.

**Reset:** "Pretend we have the full thing. What would we measure? Now — what's the fake version of the full thing that would measure the same thing?"

### Vanity metrics creep in

Team reports "we got 400 signups" or "20K impressions." These feel like progress. They don't tell you whether the assumption was right.

**Reset:** "Those are nice. What did the signups DO after they signed up? What's the behavioral metric we're actually trying to move?"

### Scope has expanded since last share-out

They started testing one assumption. Now they're also redesigning the onboarding flow, exploring a new segment, and pitching a pricing change. Three experiments in a trench coat.

**Reset:** "Pick the one riskiest assumption. Which of these is it? The others go back in the queue until we know that one."

## The reset kit

When you see overbuild, fall back to these questions. They work in any of the above cases.

1. **"What's the one thing we still don't know about the customer?"** — cuts scope.
2. **"Can three customer conversations this week answer it?"** — resets to rapid.
3. **"What's the smallest version of this we can finish by Friday?"** — forces a timebox.
4. **"If customers do X, we keep going. If they do Y, we kill it. What are X and Y?"** — forces kill criteria.

## The pretotype principle

From Alberto Savoia via Ries: the fastest experiments fake the product entirely. Concierge MVPs, manual backends, landing-page tests, Wizard of Oz. Your team's bias will usually be toward building; your bias should be toward faking until the customer forces you to build.

When the team says "we need to build this to test it," say: "What would the pretotype look like? What can we fake this week instead?"

## Why this matters for the leader

Every week spent overbuilding an experiment is a week not spent on the next cycle. Innovation velocity is cycle time. If your team runs one cycle a quarter, you'll have 4 data points a year. If they run one a week, you'll have 50. The leader's job is to protect weekly cadence, not to approve bigger and bigger builds.

If the team defends the overbuild ("we really need to be thorough here"), ask: "What's the cost of being thorough this week vs. the cost of being wrong for six weeks because we didn't get feedback?" Usually the answer answers itself.

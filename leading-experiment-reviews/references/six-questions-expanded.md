# The Six Questions, Expanded

The six questions from the main SKILL.md, with worked examples of how a team might answer and what you do next. Use this when you have a few minutes to prep before a share-out, or when you're reviewing how the conversation actually went.

---

## 1. What was the riskiest assumption you were testing?

**Good answer:** "We assumed customers would pay a variable monthly price without needing to forecast it. If that's wrong, our whole pricing model breaks."

**Your next move:** "Good. Did you actually test that one, or did you test something easier?"

**Weak answer:** "We tested whether the feature works." (That's a usability test, not a business-model assumption.)

**Your next move:** "What would have to be true about the customer for the business to work? Was that what you tested?"

---

## 2. What did customers actually DO?

**Good answer:** "Three out of five opened our calendar link. Two of those three scheduled a follow-up call. One of those two sent us a contract template without being asked."

**Your next move:** Nothing yet. You got behavior. Now ask question 3.

**Weak answer:** "They said they loved it." or "They seemed excited."

**Your next move:** "Got it — but what did they DO? Did anyone book time with you? Send a contract? Commit to a pilot? If 'love it' isn't backed by an action, it's not signal."

---

## 3. What surprised you?

**Good answer:** "I expected CFOs to care most about forecasting. Three out of five didn't mention it. They all talked about how the finance team sees them as a cost center."

**Your next move:** "That's worth more than the data we were after. What does that change about the next cycle?"

**Weak answer:** "Nothing really surprised us. Things went about as we expected."

**Your next move:** "That's a yellow flag. Either we already knew the answer (so why run the experiment?) or we're filtering out the surprises. Let's look at the raw notes together."

---

## 4. What would have changed your mind?

**Good answer:** "If fewer than two out of five had shown up to the call, we were going to kill this branch of the canvas and pivot the channel."

**Your next move:** "Good. You had kill criteria. What about the other direction — what result would have made you more aggressive?"

**Weak answer:** "I don't know — we would have figured it out."

**Your next move:** "That's the problem. Without knowing what would change your mind, the experiment couldn't have failed — and an experiment that can't fail isn't an experiment. Let's define that for next cycle up front."

---

## 5. What's blocking you from the next cycle?

**Good answer:** "We need access to two more customers in the roofing segment. Our contact list is tapped."

**Your next move:** "Let me make three intros by tomorrow. Anything else?"

**Weak answer:** "We're not sure what to do next."

**Your next move:** "Based on what you learned, what would you do next if you had to start tomorrow?" (Don't answer for them. Let them work it.)

---

## 6. What do you need from me?

**Good answer:** "A decision on whether we can redirect the other team's contract-review work to unblock our pricing test, and cover for Sandra who's out next week."

**Your next move:** Answer both or commit to a date. Do it in the room.

**Weak answer:** "Nothing, I think we're good."

**Your next move:** "Okay — but I'm going to watch for places I can clear things for you. If something slows you down this week, ping me directly."

---

## Fast twelve — additional questions for deeper cuts

If the six don't surface what you need, pull from these (from the canon — Ries, Blank, Torres, Bland, Fitzpatrick, Bezos, Edmondson, Cagan):

- "What did you learn?" (not what did you ship)
- "Who exactly is the customer, and what problem are they hiring you to solve?"
- "What's the smallest test that could kill this idea?"
- "How would you have known you were wrong?"
- "What evidence would change your mind right now?"
- "What outcome are you moving, and by how much?"
- "If this succeeds, what breaks?"
- "Is this a Type 1 (irreversible) or Type 2 (reversible) decision?"
- "Did they tell you about their life, or react to your pitch?"
- "What would have to be true for this to work?"
- "What's the cost of being wrong about this for another month?"
- "What are we not saying in this room?"

Full attributions in `poking-holes-in-experiments.md`.

## One rule

When in doubt, ask the next question. Don't give the answer. The team owning the answer is the whole point.

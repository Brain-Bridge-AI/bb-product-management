# Poking Holes in Experiments

The leader's job in a share-out is to test whether the team tested well. This reference gives you the question bank, the team responses to watch for, and the fast cuts that do the most work.

## The fast twelve — questions that do the most work

Pick two or three based on what the team just said. Do not fire them all. Most of these take 30 seconds to ask and surface 20 minutes of real thinking.

1. **"What did you learn?"** — Not what you shipped, not what you did. Learning is the output. *(Ries)*
2. **"Who exactly is the customer, and what problem are they hiring you to solve?"** — Forces a real answer, not a segment label. *(Blank)*
3. **"What was the riskiest assumption you were testing — and did you actually test it?"** — Watch for "we decided to test X because it was easier." *(Bland, Osterwalder)*
4. **"What would have to be true for this to work?"** — Surfaces hidden dependencies. *(Roger Martin, Amazon 6-pager)*
5. **"What did customers DO — not what did they say?"** — The core Torres / Fitzpatrick move. Past behavior over stated intent. *(Torres)*
6. **"Did they tell you about their life, or react to your pitch?"** — The Mom Test filter. *(Fitzpatrick)*
7. **"What's the smallest test that could have killed this idea?"** — If they didn't run that, why not? *(Maurya, Bland)*
8. **"How would you have known you were wrong?"** — Falsifiability check. If there's no answer, it wasn't a hypothesis. *(Ries)*
9. **"What evidence would change your mind right now?"** — Tests whether they can still update. *(Edmondson)*
10. **"What outcome are you moving, and by how much?"** — Keeps the team anchored to measurable customer change, not feature output. *(Cagan, Perri)*
11. **"If this succeeds, what breaks?"** — Second-order thinking. Forces them to think past the win. *(Bezos)*
12. **"Is this a Type 1 (irreversible) or Type 2 (reversible) decision?"** — If Type 2, stop debating and go. *(Bezos)*

## The team responses to watch for

When the team presents, listen for these patterns. Each one is a hole you can poke with a follow-up question.

### "Customers loved it"

They're reporting compliments, not evidence. This is the most common and most dangerous response.

**Follow up:** "What did they do after they loved it? Did anyone commit to anything — money, time, a follow-up meeting, an intro?"

### "We would probably…"

They're describing what they might do, not what they did. Hypothetical future behavior is fiction.

**Follow up:** "Stay with the past. What actually happened in the experiment? What did customers do?"

### "We're going to build the real thing to test it"

They're skipping the rapid experiment and going straight to product. You've lost speed.

**Follow up:** "What's the smallest test that could kill this idea before we build anything? Five customers, one prototype on paper — what would that tell you?"

### "We validated it" (after 1-2 conversations)

Sample size too small, or evidence too thin.

**Follow up:** "How many customers? What specifically did they do that made you sure? If the next three we talk to contradict this, do we still think it's validated?"

### "We think customers want…"

They're speculating. This is a hypothesis, not a finding.

**Follow up:** "What did the test actually show? What's the evidence, separate from what you think it means?"

### "We need more time / more people / more data"

Sometimes legitimate, often a stall. The cost of another cycle is usually less than the cost of not running it.

**Follow up:** "What's the smallest version you could finish by [next Friday]? What would we know then that we don't know now?"

### "The experiment didn't really work — we had [technical / scheduling / data] problems"

Infrastructure excuses. Sometimes real; usually a dodge.

**Follow up:** "Separate from the execution problems — what did the partial data show? Where did you get surprised?"

## The two hardest cases

### When the team ran a clean experiment and the result contradicts what you believe

Your job is to update. Follow the evidence, not your gut. Ask: "If I take this data at face value, what does it imply we should do next?" If you override it, you've trained them to bring you evidence that confirms your priors, and the whole system collapses.

### When the team ran a bad experiment that happened to produce a result you like

Don't take the win. A flattering result from a flawed test is worse than a disappointing result from a rigorous one, because it anchors you on a false read. Say so: "I like what this shows, but the test doesn't actually let us conclude that. What would a cleaner test look like?"

## One rule for the whole interaction

When you're about to offer an answer, turn it into a question first. Nine times out of ten the team already knows, and your asking instead of telling keeps them owning the problem.

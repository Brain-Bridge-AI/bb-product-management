# Vision vs. UVP vs. Mission vs. Tagline

These four artifacts are all short statements about a company or product, and they all get confused with each other constantly. Walk through this before drafting to make sure you're writing the right thing.

## Quick decoder

| Artifact | Audience | Timeframe | Purpose |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Mission** | All employees, public | 50+ years, stable | Why the company exists at all |
| **Vision** | Team, recruits | 3+ years, stable-ish | What future we're building for the customer |
| **UVP** | Target customer | Current | Why a specific customer should pick us over alternatives |
| **Tagline** | Prospects browsing | Current | Catch attention / be memorable |

## Worked example — same company, all four

Imagine a BB offering that helps fractional CFOs manage their feast-and-famine cycle.

**Mission** (why the company exists):
> "To help small-business leaders have meaningful impact without burning out."

**Vision** (the future we're building for the customer):
> "Fractional leaders spend half their week on strategy, not cleanup."

**UVP** (why a fractional CFO should pick this specific product):
> "The only workspace that turns your client chaos into a forecastable pipeline — without another tool to check every morning."

**Tagline** (catchy memorable thing on the website):
> "Pipeline, finally steady."

All four describe the same company. Each does a different job. If you write the wrong one in the wrong slot, nothing works.

## Common confusions and fixes

**"Our vision is to be the #1 X."**
That's a competitive-position goal, not a vision. Rewrite it as the customer's changed world.

**"Our mission is to help customers do Y."**
That's a vision (or a UVP) dressed as a mission. Missions answer "why do we exist at all?" — they're bigger than any product.

**"Our vision is [clever alliterative phrase]."**
That's a tagline. Visions guide team decisions; taglines catch attention. Different jobs, different constraints.

**"Our UVP is [aspirational future statement]."**
That's a vision. UVPs answer "why this over the alternatives right now?" — they compare you to specific competing options your customer is weighing today.

## Rule of thumb

When in doubt, ask what the statement is *for*:

- If it's for an engineer deciding whether to join the team → vision.
- If it's for a prospect on a landing page deciding whether to click → tagline or UVP.
- If it's for a journalist writing about why the company exists → mission.
- If it's for a customer comparing you to a specific competitor → UVP.

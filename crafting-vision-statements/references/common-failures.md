# Common Failure Patterns

Seven patterns that show up repeatedly when teams draft vision statements. Walk through the list during refinement; if the candidate matches a pattern, rewrite before moving on.

## 1. Feature list in disguise

**Symptom:** The vision names specific product features or capabilities.

- ❌ "A platform that uses AI to auto-generate marketing campaigns for small businesses."
- ✅ "Small business owners spend zero time worrying about how to reach their next customer."

**Why it fails:** features change, the vision shouldn't. Strip the mechanism.

## 2. Revenue or growth goal masquerading as vision

**Symptom:** The statement contains numbers about the business, not numbers about the customer.

- ❌ "Reach $100M ARR by 2030."
- ❌ "Be the #1 CRM for roofing contractors."
- ✅ "Every roofing contractor makes confident bids in under five minutes."

**Why it fails:** revenue goals and competitive position are outcomes for you, not the customer. Shift the subject.

## 3. Buzzword soup

**Symptom:** The statement uses abstract business language that could apply to any company.

- ❌ "Empower organizations to unlock transformative value through intelligent solutions."
- ✅ "Fractional CFOs bill 40% more billable hours without working more weekends."

**Why it fails:** no one can picture it. Replace every buzzword with a concrete customer-world detail.

## 4. Not audacious enough (fails the recruiting test)

**Symptom:** The vision describes an incremental improvement over today. No stretch.

- ❌ "Help marketers write better emails faster."
- ✅ "No marketer is ever surprised by what their customers open."

**Why it fails:** a top engineer won't leave their job for an incremental improvement. Push the future state further out.

## 5. Too tactical (fails the pivot test)

**Symptom:** The vision is tied to a specific pricing model, channel, or business-model mechanism.

- ❌ "A subscription service that delivers weekly meal plans to busy parents."
- ✅ "Busy parents never wonder what's for dinner."

**Why it fails:** if you pivot from subscription to marketplace tomorrow, the vision breaks. Abstract from the mechanism.

## 6. No customer in the vision (fails the hypothesis test)

**Symptom:** The vision is about the company, the team, or the product — not the customer.

- ❌ "Build the world's most elegant roofing software."
- ✅ "Every roofing owner feels like their back-office runs itself."

**Why it fails:** you can't trace team decisions back to a vision that doesn't name a customer. The customer must be the implied subject.

## 7. Marketing slogan bleed

**Symptom:** The statement reads like billboard copy — clever, alliterative, designed to be heard by prospects rather than worked toward by teams.

- ❌ "Finance forward. Future focused."
- ✅ "Fractional CFOs spend half their time on strategy, not cleanup."

**Why it fails:** a marketing slogan's job is to catch attention. A vision's job is to guide decisions. The two have opposite constraints. If the statement sounds catchy, ask: does this actually help the team prioritize? If not, strip the cleverness.

## The "before/after" move

When a candidate vision fails one of these patterns, the fix usually follows a consistent shape:

1. Find the non-customer subject in the sentence (the product, the company, the revenue).
2. Replace it with the customer.
3. Describe the *changed state* of the customer's world, not the *change* being made to them.

**Example walkthrough:**
- Start: "We build AI tools that help small businesses automate their back office."
- After step 1: customer = small business owner.
- After step 2: "Small business owners..."
- After step 3: "...spend their days growing, not grinding through paperwork."

That's the shape of the fix across all seven patterns.

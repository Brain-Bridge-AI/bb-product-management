# Richer Artifact Forms

The one-liner vision is the right output for a 5-minute workshop exercise or an interview opener. For a multi-year product or company bet, the one-liner distills a richer artifact — it doesn't replace it. This reference covers the four most useful richer forms and when to choose each.

## When a one-liner is enough

- Workshop Practice C (5-minute exercise).
- Problem-interview opener (vision as anchor, not artifact).
- Sticky-note on a team wall.
- First draft of anything — start here, expand only if needed.

## When you need a richer form

- Recruiting senior engineers / designers / PMs — they want to see the future, not just a slogan.
- Investor pitches — investors weigh the size of the ambition against the team's ability to reach it.
- Multi-team alignment — when 3+ teams need to coordinate toward the same future, shared understanding of the customer's future world has to survive translation.
- Company-level or product-level bets with a 5+ year arc.

## The four forms

### 1. Cagan narrative / vision demo

**Format:** 10-15 minute story, slide deck, or concept video showing the customer's future world. Walks through a day in the life after the product succeeds.

**Strengths:** Best at recruiting missionaries. Engineers can picture what they'd be building. Investors can picture the category.

**Weaknesses:** Expensive to produce. Can become outdated as strategy pivots — revisit annually.

**Use when:** building an ambitious product-level vision you need to recruit a senior team around. Cagan's canonical example is the "Sun Microsystems Starfire" video from 1994 — 16 minutes of future-of-work fiction that still holds up.

### 2. Amazon PR/FAQ

**Format:** Write the press release announcing the product's launch — *before you build it*. Then write the FAQ anticipating customer and internal questions.

**Structure:**
- **Headline** — one line the customer would care about.
- **Subheading** — the customer benefit in plain language.
- **Problem paragraph** — what world the customer is living in today.
- **Solution paragraph** — the changed world.
- **Customer quote** — one sentence from a specific persona.
- **FAQ** — anticipated questions, especially the uncomfortable ones.

**Strengths:** Forces customer-obsessed specificity. Surfaces weak thinking — if you can't write a compelling press release, the idea isn't there yet.

**Weaknesses:** Easy to write a polished PR/FAQ for a bad idea. Works best with pressure-testing from skeptics.

**Use when:** you're entering a new product area and need to decide whether it's worth investing in at all.

### 3. Pichler Product Vision Board

**Format:** 5-cell visual board.

| Cell | Content |
|---|---|
| Vision | The one-liner. |
| Target Group | Specific customer segment(s). |
| Needs | The core customer needs the product addresses. |
| Product | Category + key features (at the vision level, not the roadmap level). |
| Business Goals | How the company wins if the vision lands. |

**Strengths:** Everything on one page. Forces alignment across product, customer, and business. Good shared artifact for product teams.

**Weaknesses:** Can feel static once filled. Needs explicit revisit cadence.

**Use when:** a product team needs a single shared reference and the vision needs to sit alongside segment/needs/business context.

### 4. Biddle DHM

**Format:** Three sentences, tightly coupled.

- **Delight customers** — how will the product delight (be specific).
- **in Hard-to-copy ways** — what's the moat (network effects, brand, proprietary data, scale, etc.).
- **that produce Margin** — how does the business win.

**Strengths:** Couples vision to competitive moat. Surfaces vision-level strategy gaps (e.g., "we delight customers, but copycats will eat us in 18 months").

**Weaknesses:** Demands proxy metrics earlier than other forms — some teams aren't ready to commit.

**Use when:** the team has strong product instincts but is weak on strategic defensibility. DHM forces the moat conversation.

## Choosing between forms

| If the goal is... | Use... |
|---|---|
| Recruiting engineers | Cagan narrative / demo |
| Deciding whether to enter a space | Amazon PR/FAQ |
| Aligning a product team | Pichler Vision Board |
| Pressure-testing the moat | Biddle DHM |
| All of the above | All four, in that order |

## Relationship to the one-liner

Whichever richer form you use, always distill it back to a one-liner for everyday use. The richer artifact tells the full story; the one-liner is the handle the team grabs when making a tough call at 4pm on a Thursday. Both live side-by-side — keep them in sync.

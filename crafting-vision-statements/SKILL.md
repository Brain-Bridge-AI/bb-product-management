# Crafting Vision Statements

## What this skill is for

A **vision statement** is a short, aspirational description of the future your product or team aims to create — for your customer. It's the team's north star. It names the destination, not the path. Strategy and experiments change; the vision stays stable.

It is NOT:
- A UVP (that's customer-facing product positioning — different thing, lives on the Lean Canvas).
- A mission statement (broader organizational purpose — "why the company exists").
- A tagline or marketing slogan (customer-acquisition copy).
- A product description, feature list, or roadmap.
- A revenue goal or competitive-position statement ("be the #1 X in Y").

Think of the vision as the answer to: **"What does the customer's world look like after we succeed?"**

## Timeframe: 3+ years

Far enough out that the team has to stretch to get there. Close enough that it still feels possible — people can imagine the world of the vision, not just wish for it.

The canon is split on exact timeframes: Cagan says 3-10 years for product vision; Biddle says 5 years coupled with a moat; Collins's BHAG runs 10-30 years at the company level. All of them agree on the shape — long enough that strategy will pivot multiple times within the window, short enough that individuals working today can picture themselves in the future state.

**Default to 3+ years.** If the horizon is under 18 months, you're drafting a roadmap milestone or an OKR, not a vision. If it's over 20 years, you're drafting a BHAG or core purpose, not a product vision.

## Vision as hypothesis

**The vision is a hypothesis, not a decree.** It's your current best bet about what future will matter most to your customer. As customer discovery and experiments return evidence, the vision itself must be testable — can every team decision trace back to a customer opportunity that ladders up to the vision? If not, either the decisions are off-mission or the vision is wrong.

This is the Teresa Torres framing: vision is continuously pressure-tested by the opportunity solution tree. It's not graven in stone; it's the top-level hypothesis in a stack of hypotheses.

**Practical implication:** revisit the vision at least annually, and when major experiment evidence contradicts it. Most teams change their vision too rarely (treating it as sacred) or too often (whiplashing on the first contrary data point). Aim for the middle: stable by default, revisable on strong evidence.

## The three components

Every strong vision statement has all three. Missing any one produces a vision that sounds nice but fails in the wild.

### 1. Simple

Short enough to be memorable. Captures the *impact* you want to make on the customer's life, not the mechanism by which you'll make it.

### 2. Customer-Focused

Describes the customer's changed world. Not your solution, not your revenue, not your product, not your competitive position. The customer is the subject.

### 3. Motivating

Teams want to wake up and work to achieve it. If it doesn't stir anyone, it's not a vision — it's a requirements doc.

**Rule:** This is an anchoring point for your team, not a marketing slogan. If it reads like it belongs on a billboard, you've drifted. Pull back to team-anchor territory.

## The template

Most strong visions fit one of two patterns:

**Pattern A — Outcome for customer (tightest, most memorable):**
> "[Specific customer outcome]."

Example: "1,000 songs in your pocket." — iPod.

**Pattern B — "I believe I can help [segment] [outcome]":**
> "I believe I can help [specific customer segment] [specific outcome]."

Example: "I believe I can help fractional leaders bring AI into small businesses without runaway costs."

Pattern A is tighter and more memorable; Pattern B is better when you need to name the segment explicitly — especially for problem-interview openers (see `running-problem-interviews`).

## Benchmark library

Read these out loud. They're all famous for a reason. Use them as the bar.

| Company | Vision | Why it works |
|---|---|---|
| **iPod** | "1,000 songs in your pocket." | Concrete outcome. Zero product jargon. Visualizable. |
| **Disney** | "To make people happy." | Radical simplicity. Customer is the subject. |
| **Google** | "Access to the world's information in one click." | Scope + customer benefit, mechanism-agnostic. |
| **Ikea** | "Create a better everyday life." | Abstract but emotionally motivating; customer-centered. |
| **Ford** (early) | "Democratize the automobile." | One word does all the work. Clear future state. |

Anti-examples (what NOT to do):
- ❌ "Be the world's leading AI platform for small business." (Product-centric, competitive-position, not customer-focused.)
- ❌ "Help companies scale through intelligent automation solutions that drive ROI." (Buzzword soup. Neither simple nor motivating.)
- ❌ "Ship the best product in our category." (Internal/competitive. Not about the customer.)
- ❌ "Reach $100M ARR by 2030." (Revenue goal, not a vision.)

See `references/common-failures.md` for more patterns.

## The three tests (use these to pressure-test every draft)

These are the load-bearing checks. A vision that passes all three is ready; one that fails any is not.

### 1. Recruiting test

**"Would a top engineer (or designer, or PM) leave their current job to work on this?"**

Vision's job is to recruit missionaries, not mercenaries. If your strongest candidate reads the vision and shrugs, you don't have a vision — you have a job description. Borrowed from Cagan and a16z.

If the vision fails this test, usually the fix is audacity: the outcome isn't big enough, or the customer impact isn't sharp enough, or the future state doesn't feel meaningfully better than today.

### 2. Pivot test

**"If we pivot the business model tomorrow — new pricing, new channel, new segment — does the vision still hold?"**

Vision is the constant; strategy pivots. If changing the business model invalidates the vision, it's not a vision, it's a plan. Borrowed from Ries and Maurya.

If the vision fails this test, usually the fix is abstraction: the statement is too tied to a specific mechanism (e.g., "subscription-based access to X" — subscription is a pricing model, not a vision).

### 3. Vision-as-hypothesis test

**"Can every team decision trace to a customer opportunity that ladders to the vision?"**

Vision is falsifiable. If you can't trace the last five prioritization decisions up to it, either the team is off-mission or the vision is too vague to guide decisions. Borrowed from Torres / continuous discovery.

If the vision fails this test, usually the fix is specificity: the customer isn't named concretely enough, or the outcome is so abstract ("empower people") that any decision can claim to ladder up to it.

## The crafting process

### Step 1 — Brainstorm 2-3 candidates quickly

Don't agonize over the first draft. Write 2-3 candidates fast. In a workshop, this is a 5-minute forced-function, not a polishing session.

For each candidate, answer: **"What does my customer's world look like 3+ years from now if I succeed?"**

### Step 2 — Choose the strongest

Read each aloud. Which one would survive a skeptical engineer asking "why does this matter?" Which one could you still believe in after shipping three product iterations?

### Step 3 — Refine for clarity and impact

Check the candidate against the three components:
- [ ] Simple — can you say it in one breath?
- [ ] Customer-Focused — is the customer (not you) the implied subject?
- [ ] Motivating — does it stir anything?

### Step 4 — Run the three tests

- [ ] Recruiting: would a top engineer leave their job for this?
- [ ] Pivot: does it survive a business-model pivot?
- [ ] Hypothesis: can every recent prioritization decision trace to it?

If it fails any test, iterate. Common fixes: more audacious (recruiting), more abstract from mechanism (pivot), more specific customer (hypothesis).

## Richer artifact forms (when you have more than 5 minutes)

The one-liner is the right output for a workshop or an interview opener. For a multi-year product bet, consider a richer form:

- **Cagan narrative / vision demo** — a 10-15 minute story or concept video of the customer's future world. Best for recruiting engineers and investors.
- **Amazon PR/FAQ** — write the launch press release before building, then the FAQ. Forces customer-obsessed specificity.
- **Pichler Product Vision Board** — 5 cells (vision, target group, needs, product, business goals). Good for shared team alignment.
- **Biddle DHM** — Delight customers, in Hard-to-copy ways, that produce Margin. Couples the vision with the moat.

The one-liner distills these; it doesn't replace them. For company-level or product-level bets, do both — a richer artifact AND a distilled one-liner for everyday use.

## Scope levels

A vision statement can operate at three scope levels. The process is the same; the timeframe and audience shift.

| Scope | Timeframe | Audience | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Company** | 10+ years | All employees, investors | "Democratize the automobile." (Ford) |
| **Product** | 3-10 years | Product team, partners | "1,000 songs in your pocket." (iPod) |
| **Initiative / Feature** | 1-3 years | Specific team / workstream | "No customer loses a storm job to a slower competitor." (Home-Resto sales tool) |

When a user asks for "a vision", ask which scope they mean. Workshop Practice C is usually initiative-level; a new BB offering is product-level; the BB company itself is company-level.

## Integration with other skills

- **`populating-lean-canvas`** — The Lean Canvas doesn't have a Vision box, but the vision sits above the canvas as the anchor. Draft the canvas first if you haven't; the vision often crystallizes after Customer Segment and Problem are clear.
- **`running-problem-interviews`** — The vision IS the interview opener. When you're about to interview a prospective customer, the vision is what you say to anchor them on their own world without pitching a solution. See `vision-statement.md` reference in that skill.
- **`running-rapid-experiments`** — The vision is the top-of-loop anchor. When experiments return contradictory evidence, the vision is what helps the team decide whether to pivot *within* the vision (iterate) or question the vision itself.

## Workshop usage (P&E Leadership Training Practice C)

- **Timebox:** 5 minutes.
- **Output:** One vision statement per team, written on a sticky or flipchart.
- **Share-out:** Each team reads theirs aloud; coach challenges against the three components and at least one of the three tests.
- **Refinement in place:** If it fails a component or test, rewrite on the spot rather than discussing it abstractly.

The point of the exercise is not a perfect vision — it's the muscle of putting the customer's 3+ year future state into words the team can rally around.

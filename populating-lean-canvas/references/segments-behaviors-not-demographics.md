# Customer Segments: Behaviors, Not Demographics

For new products, Customer Segments must be filled with **situations and observable behaviors** — not demographic categories. Demographics are shorthand for behaviors, and the shorthand hides the real qualifier.

## Why this matters

When someone writes "our product is for 50-year-olds," they're not really saying their product is for anyone who happens to be 50. They're saying they believe 50-year-olds share a specific attribute or behavior. Almost always it's something like "not tech-savvy" or "has disposable income" or "worries about retirement."

Write the real attribute. Not the age.

Same goes for:

- **"Enterprise companies"** → often shorthand for "has procurement processes," "runs on annual budgets," or "needs SOC 2 compliance." Say which one.
- **"Millennials"** → often shorthand for "prefers self-serve onboarding" or "researches purchases on mobile." Say which.
- **"Small business owners"** → often shorthand for "wears multiple hats," "can't afford full-time specialists," or "makes buying decisions alone." Say which.
- **"Fortune 500"** → often shorthand for "has legacy integrations," "requires vendor review," or "has revenue >$5B." Say which.

## How to unpack in-session

When the user writes a demographic in the Segments box, ask:

> "What is it about [the demographic] that makes them a good fit for this? What behavior or attribute are you actually thinking of?"

Capture their answer. Write the behavior in the box, not the demographic. If the user can't articulate the behavior, that's a signal — the demographic was a placeholder for something they haven't actually thought through, and the real Segments box is empty until they do.

## The reverse also matters

Sometimes a real attribute looks like a demographic but isn't. "Organizations with 50-500 employees" might be a real scale-band signal (too small for enterprise tools, too big for solo tools). Don't reflexively strip numbers — ask what the number means operationally, and capture the operational meaning.

## What good Segments content looks like

Attributes and behaviors the anchor customer demonstrates or could be observed demonstrating:

- "Paying for multiple overlapping software tools and not getting value from any of them" (behavior, observable)
- "Has salespeople in the field who are commission-compensated" (attribute, observable)
- "Makes software purchase decisions in <90 days when a board meeting is approaching" (behavior, observable + situational)
- "Fractional C-level executives brokering technology decisions across multiple engagements" (attribute, operational)

Not:

- "Mid-size companies" (demographic shorthand — unpack it)
- "Forward-thinking teams" (not observable)
- "50-500 employees" (maybe real if it maps to a budget tier or decision process — ask)

## Early adopter inside the segment

Segments has two layers: the broader segment and the specific early adopter within it. Name both, and fill the entire canvas for the early adopter — not the broader market.

Early adopters are identified by pain intensity + MVP tolerance (the rip-it-out-of-my-hands test). An early-adopter profile typically has additional situational signals:

- Compliance or regulatory requirement forcing urgency
- Forward-leaning on technology / wants to be first
- Already spending on similar tools and not getting value
- Belief that a bigger opportunity is being missed
- Strong internal champion pushing for change

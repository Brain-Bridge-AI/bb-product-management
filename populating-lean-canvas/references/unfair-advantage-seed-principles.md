# Unfair Advantage: Seed Mode

Unfair Advantage is the second seed-mode box (after UVP). Users commonly pad this box with plausible-but-stretchy claims, so the skill supplies the principles + draft candidates and pushes back on padding.

## Why Unfair Advantage is seed-mode

Everyone wants to believe they have an unfair advantage. Most founders pad this box with claims like "great team," "passion," "first mover," or "better product" — none of which are unfair in any meaningful sense.

The skill's job is to introduce a real definition, apply it rigorously, and offer "none yet" as a defensible answer when padding would be the alternative.

## The canonical definition

**Jason Cohen's rule:** A real unfair advantage is **something that cannot be easily copied or bought.**

If a competitor with capital and a weekend could replicate it, it's not unfair. If another founder could hire someone like you, it's not unfair. If it can be acquired through normal business channels, it's not unfair.

## The "lots of people like that" disqualifier

A simpler, user-facing version of the Cohen rule:

> "Does your candidate describe a category with many people in it? If yes, it's not unfair."

Examples:

- "I have 30 years of tech experience" → lots of people like that. Not unfair.
- "We have a great team" → lots of teams describe themselves that way. Not unfair.
- "We were first to market" → almost always matched within 18 months. Not unfair.
- "I'm a former McKinsey consultant" → lots of people like that. Not unfair.

Apply this test aggressively. The point isn't to be harsh — it's to prevent the box from becoming decorative.

## Common false unfair advantages (reject these)

- **"Great team"** — universal claim, never unfair
- **"Passion / drive / hustle"** — not a moat
- **"First mover"** — almost never durable at scale
- **"Better product"** — copyable, especially in software
- **Press / awards / funding** — not durable, not a moat
- **Generic tech adjectives** ("AI-powered", "proprietary algorithm") — meaningless until specified
- **"We've been at this longer"** — tenure isn't moat; what you built with tenure might be
- **"Domain expertise"** — usually a category with many members; push for specificity

## Real unfair advantages (accept if honest)

These can be durable, though each has specific conditions:

- **Insider information** — deep industry experience producing knowledge outsiders can't acquire without living the role. Specific to a domain, not general.
- **Earned authority / personal brand** in a specific segment — built over years of public work, not bought. Must be specific to the target customer segment.
- **The right endorsements** — specific authority figures who actively champion, not generic advisors on a slide.
- **Exclusive data** — proprietary dataset competitors cannot practically or legally acquire. Conditional: you must actually harvest and apply it.
- **Community moat** — existing community the product sits inside, where community members introduce new members.
- **SEO / content compound** — years of ranked content that can't be replicated with money, only time.
- **Regulatory position** — licenses, certifications, FDA approvals, etc. Industry-specific.
- **Switching costs / network effects** baked into the product — data lock-in, multi-party network value, integration depth.
- **Named talent** the competitor specifically cannot hire — domain-specific celebrities, not "great team."

## "None yet" is a valid answer

If none of the real advantages apply, the honest answer is "none yet."

This is often the correct answer for early-stage products. It's NOT a failure — it's a clear-eyed assessment that says "we need to build one." Padding the box with weak claims hides that reality.

Unfair Advantage evolves. Executing on Channels, Revenue, and the data loop over time builds brand, community, and exclusive data. A product that launches with "none yet" can legitimately have a real unfair advantage two years later.

## How to draft candidates

Draft 2-4 candidates grounded in this session's elicited content:

1. Look at Segments for clues about earned domain authority or insider information.
2. Look at Solution for clues about exclusive data loops or switching costs.
3. Look at the user's own background (if relevant context exists) for specific endorsements or named talent.
4. **Always include "none yet" as an explicit candidate** with a short rationale. This gives the user a clean escape from padding.

Tag each candidate with a rationale + a honest assessment of durability:

- **"Real if X, fragile if Y"** — state the conditions.
- **"Looks real but fails the Cohen test because Z"** — name why it doesn't qualify.

## How to run the seed interaction

1. Switch modes explicitly: "Unfair Advantage is another one where most people don't have strong instincts. Let me supply the principles and draft some candidates."
2. Present Cohen's rule + the "lots of people like that" test.
3. Present the padding-to-reject list (short form).
4. Present 2-4 candidates + "none yet" as an explicit option.
5. Ask the user to react. Push back on padding if they lean into weak candidates.
6. If the final answer is "none yet," frame it positively: "That's a clear signal to focus on building one — through the data loop, community, or earned authority over time."

## Common failure modes

- **Accepting the first plausible candidate.** If it passes "lots of people like that," push back.
- **Skipping the Cohen test on candidates that sound good.** A candidate isn't unfair just because it's true — it has to be uncopiable and unbuyable.
- **Treating "none yet" as shameful.** It's the correct and honest answer for most early-stage products.
- **Padding with the user's resume.** Personal tenure is rarely unfair (lots of people like that). What was BUILT with the tenure can be unfair — specific relationships, specific accumulated data, specific community — but not the tenure itself.

# Problem: Three Sub-Fields

The Problem box is NOT one field. It's three, and filling only one (usually just "pains") produces a hollow canvas.

## The three sub-fields

### 1. Pains / what's getting in the way

The specific things that hurt, break, cost money, waste time, or block progress for the anchor customer. The stuff the customer actively complains about, works around, or pretends isn't a problem.

**Fill by brainstorming widely.** Every pain you heard, plus every pain you believe is likely given what you know. Then winnow — star the probable top pain.

**Verbatim quotes are gold.** If the customer said "my chief of staff spends Fridays chasing people for updates," that exact phrase goes in the box. Don't summarize.

### 2. Desired outcomes

What the customer wants the world to look like AFTER the problem is solved. The positive-frame version of the pains.

This is the sub-field most practitioners skip. Skipping it means the canvas only sees the negative frame and misses what success looks like from the customer's perspective.

Desired outcomes are often heard less directly than pains — customers talk about what hurts more than what they dream of. If you're inferring the desired outcomes (not hearing them), tag `[assumed]` honestly.

Distinct from "inverse of pain." Pain: "salespeople don't enter data." Desired outcome might be "I can see what's happening in the field without chasing people," or "my commission dollars generate measurable value," or "we stop leaving money on the table." These are outcome statements, not negations.

### 3. Existing alternatives

What the customer does today to cope with the pain. This sub-field is the **most-skipped of the three**, and it's critical — it's the real competitive landscape.

Three kinds of existing alternatives:

- **Internal workarounds** — hacky processes people run to cope (spreadsheets, email threads, manual chasing, shadow systems).
- **Competitor products** — software or services that compete directly.
- **Features of tools they already own** — often the real competition, because "check if our current CRM can do this" is the first move a buyer makes.

If the existing alternative is "nothing" or "a spreadsheet," the real pain is often smaller than it looks. If the existing alternative is "we already pay three vendors to partially solve this," the pain is real and expensive.

## How to fill the box

1. Confirm anchor customer and shoe-step.
2. Brainstorm pains first (easiest entry). Dump everything heard or believed.
3. Then desired outcomes. Ask the user: "What does this customer want the world to look like once this problem is gone?" If user says "inverse of the pain," push for a more positive framing.
4. Then existing alternatives. Ask specifically about workarounds, competitors, and features-of-current-tools.
5. Tag everything with provenance. Verbatim quotes preferred.
6. Winnow: star the probable top pain. If user is unsure, that's the honest answer — star it and flag as "need to learn which pain ranks highest."

## Glance-forward discipline

When you fill Problem, you're implicitly constraining Solution and Key Metrics later:

- Every Solution item must tie back to a pain in this box.
- Every Key Metric must tie back to a pain in this box.

So the Problem box is foundational — if it's thin or wrong, the downstream boxes get thin or wrong too. Don't rush it.

## Common failure modes

- **Only filling pains.** Skipping desired outcomes and existing alternatives.
- **Paraphrasing instead of quoting.** Losing the customer's language.
- **Over-abstracting pains.** "Operational inefficiency" is not a pain — it's a category label. Specific: "The chief of staff spends Fridays manually chasing field reps for updates."
- **Listing features as pains.** "We don't have a CRM" is a missing feature, not a pain. The pain is what that missing feature costs the customer.
- **Confirmation seeking.** User wants to validate a pain they've already assumed. Push them to brainstorm broadly first, then winnow — the unexpected pains are where the unmet need often lives.

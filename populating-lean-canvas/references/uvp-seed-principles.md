# UVP: Seed Mode

UVP is one of two seed-mode boxes (the other is Unfair Advantage). Users commonly lack strong instincts here, so the skill supplies principles + drafts 2-4 session-specific candidates. The user reacts; the skill doesn't ask them to author blind.

## Why UVP is seed-mode

Most people — even experienced founders — write weak UVPs on a first pass. The failure modes are consistent (generic adjectives, feature-speak, over-abstraction), and the fix requires knowing a few canonical principles plus concrete candidates to push against.

Rather than interview the user about UVP, the skill introduces the principles, drafts candidates grounded in the Problem + Solution content already elicited, and lets the user react.

## The mental model for UVP

The UVP is a **product vision statement** — a micro-story describing the customer's world after the problem is solved. Not a sentence that names the end benefit (the textbook framing), but a brief description of what life looks like when the pain is gone.

Both framings land at the same place, but the "describe the post-solution world" frame produces stronger UVPs because it forces you to stay in the customer's view.

## Canonical principles

Present these to the user when switching into UVP:

1. **One sentence, in the customer's language.** Read it to a target customer — do they recognize themselves? If not, it's not a UVP.
2. **Names the end result the customer wants** — not a feature, not a process, not an adjective. What changes for them?
3. **Specific enough that it couldn't describe a competitor.** Swap the product name for a competitor's. Does the sentence still work? If yes, it's too generic. Kill adjective padding — "AI-powered", "smart", "simple", "seamless" are all failures.
4. **Ash Maurya's "instant clarity" scaffold** (optional, reliable):
   `<end result customer wants> + <specific period of time> + <address the key objection>`
5. **Pair with a high-level concept** — a one-line analogy in `X for Y` form. Anchors the customer fast. "Cursor for door-to-door sales." "The CRM your reps don't have to touch."

## Two formal sub-fields

The UVP box has two sub-fields. Fill both:

1. **Primary UVP sentence** — the vision-statement shape, customer-world description.
2. **High-level concept** — the X-for-Y analogy.

Don't pick between them. The skill writes both and the user keeps both.

## How to draft candidates

Draft 2-4 candidate primary UVPs grounded in the specific Problem + Solution content already elicited this session. **Candidates must be session-specific, not templates.** Pull pain language from the Problem box verbatim. Pull solution mechanisms from the Solution box. Name the key objection explicitly.

Also draft 2-3 high-level concepts (X-for-Y). Different abstraction levels — one grounded in a specific product analogy, one grounded in a metaphor, one grounded in the customer's existing mental category.

## Example of good vs. bad UVPs

**Bad (generic, feature-speak, could describe any competitor):**
- "AI-powered sales intelligence platform for modern sales teams"
- "Seamlessly integrate your sales workflow with smart automation"
- "Revolutionary CRM built for the way you work"

**Good (specific to the elicited pain, customer-language, couldn't describe a competitor):**
- "Your salespeople arrive pre-briefed and leave fully logged — Solo handles the research before the knock and the notes after."
- "Recover the revenue your field sales team is leaving on the table because the data never made it back."

The good ones reference specific behaviors (knock, arrive, leave, research, log) that come from the elicited Problem content.

## How to run the seed interaction

1. Switch modes explicitly: "Most people don't have strong instincts for UVP — let me supply the principles and draft some candidates for you to react to."
2. Present the 5 principles (short form, not a lecture).
3. Present 2-4 candidate primary UVPs, each with a one-line rationale explaining what angle it's taking.
4. Present 2-3 high-level concept candidates.
5. Ask the user to react: accept, modify, combine, or reject.
6. If they modify or combine, capture the reshaped UVP in their own words.
7. Tag provenance based on how they arrived at the final:
   - If anchored in verbatim pain language → `[heard]`
   - If based on the user's hunch about what the customer would care about → `[assumed]`

## Common failure modes

- **Unsolicited seeding.** Don't seed UVP unprompted for a user who genuinely has strong instincts. If they offer a UVP that passes the principles, honor it — seed only when they're weak or blank.
- **Generic candidates.** Candidates that could describe any product in the category are useless. Ground them in specific elicited content.
- **Candidates ignoring objections.** The Maurya scaffold includes addressing the key objection — candidates that don't name one are weaker.
- **Abandoning the high-level concept.** It's load-bearing, not optional flourish. Always write both sub-fields.
